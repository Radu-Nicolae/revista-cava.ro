Limbi Straine

Thoughts on identity

‘Your true self’; ‘my true self’. We’ve all heard those saying countless of times. They seem natural, so much so that we often use them without really considering what meaning those words hold. Do they hold any meaning? Is there such a thing as a ‘true self’? How many agonising hours have you spent asking yourself ‘Who am I?’, worrying that you don’t know yourself at all, even fearing that other people might possess more knowledge about your own person than you do?

The truth is, there is no definite ‘you’, just as there is no definite ‘me’. Perhaps we would be inclined to think that our true selves are the images we’ve each constructed of ourselves in our minds over time, that would seem like the most logical answer, right? Indeed, research has shown that memories shape a person’s identity, which would explain why people who suffer from amnesia often seem to ‘lose themselves’. It has also shown, however, that we do not access all available memory that we possess, but rather unwarily tend to select which memories to recall at any given time when creating personal narratives. When we think about ourselves, we rely on a psychological screening mechanism which, to sum it up, analyses different mental concepts, deciding which ones to label as memories. Concepts which are particularly vivid and associated with a variety of emotions are more likely to be selected as memories, but still, these possible memories must also pass a ‘test’ of plausibility, which examines whether the concept fits our general personal history, as well as the current image we have constructed of ourselves in our minds. For example, if we remember flying out to Mars, we can straight up rule out the possibility of that event being a memory. However, let’s suppose that I have always described myself as a friendly person, but after a certain distressing experience I have developed an aggressive trait which I now incorporate into my idea of myself. Now, upon being asked to describe myself, I might include events which I believe to characterise me that I would have omitted from my personal narrative before the occurrence of that certain event, for example, instances in which I acted harshly and unfriendly towards others.

For that reason, the truthfulness of our memories can be doubted. If a mental concept contains elements of the impossible (like flying out to Mars) or elements which do not fit the idea we have of ourselves, that ‘memory’ is either discarded or altered in order to be considered a memory. That goes to prove that memories are easily malleable, they can be changed and distorted without us even realising, these false memories feeling very real to us.

So then, going back to the concept of ‘identity’, what exactly is it? In my previous example I talked about describing myself as a friendly person once, but now believing that I am the opposite. In that scenario, have I acquired a new identity? Do I still possess the same identity I did before my views changed? Do I have to change my views on multiple personality traits of mine in order to for my identity to change? And if yes, on how many? Half of them? All of them? There are many flaws within all of these possibilities.

Therefore, there is no such thing as identity. There is no ‘true self’. Everything is simply a shape –an identifier with the purpose of allowing others to recognise me as ‘me’, you as ‘you’, and so on. Nothing but our shapes can be determined, limited or fixed, and trying to quantify human existence in order to obtain a false sense of identity only complicates life uselessly.
